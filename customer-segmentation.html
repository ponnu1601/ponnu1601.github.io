Customer Segmentation

541,909 transactions from a UK gift retailer, one year of data, and a single question: which customers are actually worth the most — and what should be done differently for each of them?

Chapter 01 — The Question

Who are your customers, really?

A UK online gift retailer had over half a million transactions but no systematic way to tell a loyal high-spender from a one-time buyer who drifted away. Without that distinction, marketing budgets get spread thin, retention efforts miss the mark, and the customers who actually drive revenue don't get the attention they deserve. The goal was to move from a single undifferentiated customer base to clearly defined segments — each with a different behavior profile and a different action plan.

Chapter 02 — The Method

RFM scoring, then letting K-Means find the natural groups

Each of the 4,372 customers (after data cleaning) was scored on three dimensions: Recency — how recently they last purchased, Frequency — how often they buy, and Monetary — how much they spend in total. These RFM scores were then fed into K-Means clustering, with the elbow method used to find the optimal number of segments. Four clusters emerged cleanly, each with a distinct behavioral signature that mapped directly to a business priority.

Chapter 03 — The Impact

Four segments, four completely different strategies

The VIP segment — just 11 customers out of 4,372 — each spend over £100,000 and buy more than 150 times. Losing even one of them is significant, so they warrant dedicated account management and white-glove service. At the other end, 24% of customers haven't purchased in over 8 months — they need a win-back campaign before they're gone for good. The largest group, Regular customers at 71%, are the biggest growth opportunity: they already buy, just not often enough. Targeted upsell campaigns and a loyalty program with clear progression tiers could move a meaningful portion of them toward the High-Value segment over time.
