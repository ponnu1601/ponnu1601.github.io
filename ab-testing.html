Chapter 01 — The Question

Does background color actually move the needle?

A UK retail website was considering switching its background from white to black. It sounds like a small design tweak, but design decisions like this can meaningfully affect whether a visitor converts to a buyer. The goal was to test this properly — not just look at surface-level numbers, but run a statistically rigorous experiment that could give a confident yes or no, broken down by device type and location across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

Chapter 02 — The Method

Running the full A/B pipeline end to end

5,000 users were split nearly evenly — 2,519 in the control group (white background) and 2,481 in the treatment group (black background). Three statistical tests were applied: a Z-test for proportions on conversion rates, and Mann-Whitney U tests on page views and time spent. The segment analysis checked whether the effect held consistently across all four UK regions and both mobile and desktop users — because a result that only holds for one segment isn't a result worth acting on.

Chapter 03 — The Impact

More conversions, same engagement — a clean result

The black background outperformed white in every single segment — every region, every device type — by 6 to 10 percentage points. Crucially, page views and time spent showed no statistically significant difference between the two groups (p = 0.425 and p = 0.642), meaning users browsed exactly the same way regardless of background. The effect is entirely isolated to conversion behavior. One important caveat: this dataset was created for practice purposes, so the effect size is larger than what you'd typically see in a real-world production test. Validating on live data would be the essential next step before rolling out the change.
