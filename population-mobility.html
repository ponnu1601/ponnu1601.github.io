Chapter 01 — The Question

Where does long-distance travel actually come from?

Every year, state transportation agencies make massive funding decisions — new highways, bridge upgrades, regional connectors — often guided by population estimates that don't reflect how people actually move. A densely populated county isn't necessarily a high-travel county. The real question is: which counties are generating the most long-distance trips, and can we predict that precisely enough to change how infrastructure money gets spent?

Chapter 02 — The Method

Letting the data pick the right model

Rather than assuming one algorithm would work best, 10 regression models were put to the test — from simple Linear Regression and Ridge/Lasso to SVR, Decision Trees, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, and Bagging — all trained on Bureau of Transportation Statistics trip data across all 39 counties in Washington State. Features included the full spectrum of trip distance bins (under 1 mile all the way to 500+ miles) alongside population size and urban/rural classification. The gap between approaches was stark: tree-based models captured patterns that linear methods completely missed, with Random Forest emerging as the clear winner.

Chapter 03 — The Impact

Rural counties need highways. Urban counties don't.

The findings tell a clear story. Long-distance travel rates across Washington's 39 counties span an 18.5 percentage point range — Garfield County at 22.9% on one end, King County (Seattle) at 5.1% on the other. Rural counties consistently average around 15.6% long-distance trips; urban counties sit at 6.2%. That gap isn't subtle — it's a direct signal for where regional highway investment delivers the most value versus where local transit makes more sense. At ±0.37% prediction precision, this model is accurate enough to actually move budget decisions.
