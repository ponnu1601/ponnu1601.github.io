Bird Sound Classification

Can a neural network tell birds apart just by listening? Using spectrogram images of bird calls from 12 Seattle-area species, multiple CNN architectures were built and compared — revealing when deep learning works brilliantly, and when acoustically similar species make classification genuinely difficult.

Chapter 01 — The Question

Can deep learning learn to hear what birders hear?

Experienced birders can identify dozens of species by ear alone — a skill that takes years to develop. Automating this would unlock real value for wildlife monitoring, biodiversity research, and conservation tracking. The starting point was 12 bird species common to the Seattle area, sourced from the Xeno-Canto bird recording library. The challenge: with only 36–59 samples per species, could CNNs learn meaningful acoustic patterns — or would the small dataset be a dealbreaker?

Chapter 02 — The Method

Turning sound into images, then teaching a model to see

Each 2-second audio clip was converted into a spectrogram — a visual map of sound frequencies over time — resulting in 343×256 pixel images stored in HDF5 format. Three custom CNN architectures were designed and compared: a Basic CNN, one with Dropout regularization, and one with L2 regularization. The project tackled two separate tasks: binary classification (American Crow vs House Finch) to establish a performance ceiling, and multi-class classification across all 12 species to test real-world difficulty.

Chapter 03 — The Impact

Perfect results and honest limitations

The binary classifier achieved perfect accuracy — American Crow and House Finch have acoustically distinct enough calls that a simple CNN separates them flawlessly within 3 training epochs. The multi-class task told a more nuanced story. At 70%, the regularized model was the clear winner, but species like the Red-Winged Blackbird (40% accuracy) exposed where acoustic overlap makes classification genuinely hard. The key takeaway for real-world deployment: species-specific binary detectors may outperform a single general multi-class model when the target species are known in advance.
