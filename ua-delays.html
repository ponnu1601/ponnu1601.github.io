United Airlines Departure Delay Analysis

What causes flight delays — and has that changed over 10 years? This project compares 58,665 United Airlines flights from 2013 against 153,350 from 2023–2024, using permutation testing to find out which factors still matter and which ones don't.

Chapter 01 — The Question

Do the same things still cause delays a decade later?

Airlines have invested heavily in operational improvements over the past decade — better de-icing equipment, smarter scheduling, improved weather routing. But do those investments show up in the data? The question driving this project: which factors contribute most to United Airlines departure delays from New York City airports, and have those relationships meaningfully changed between 2013 and 2023–2024? Six factors were examined — time of day, season, temperature, wind speed, precipitation, and visibility — using rigorous permutation testing to confirm which differences are statistically real rather than just noise.

Chapter 02 — The Method

Two datasets, one decade apart, tested the same way

The 2013 baseline came from the nycflights13 R package, with hourly weather already matched to flight records. For 2023–2024, 13.9 million raw BTS flight records were filtered down to 153,350 United Airlines flights at EWR, JFK, and LGA, then joined with hourly weather data from the Iowa Environmental Mesonet by airport, date, and hour. Both datasets were analyzed identically — categorical variables built the same way, the same six factors tested, and permutation tests run with 1,000 iterations each to validate that observed differences couldn't have occurred by chance.

Chapter 03 — The Impact

Winter got better. Extreme cold got dramatically worse.

The most striking finding is a split story on cold weather. Winter operations overall improved significantly — December dropped from the worst month at 64.7% delays in 2013 down to 35.0% in 2023–2024, a 13.7 point improvement. But extreme cold events — temperatures below 20°F — moved in the opposite direction, with delay rates jumping from 43.7% to 63.2%. That 19.5 point deterioration suggests that while routine winter operations have improved, truly severe cold events are now more disruptive than ever. The time-of-day snowball effect — where delays accumulate through the day — remained almost identical across both periods, as did the ranking of the top two delay factors: season first, time of day second. Precipitation, however, dropped from the third most impactful factor to fourth, suggesting real improvement in rain and snow operations.
